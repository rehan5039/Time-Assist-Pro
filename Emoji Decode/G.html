How to Use Emoji

You can use these emoji by simply copying and pasting them into the editor on any social media site, or anywhere else you'd like to use them.

Emoji are shown differently on various devices and platforms. For instance, they could look somewhat rounded on Twitter and wavy on Apple devices and Facebook. On Windows, some country flags might not function and might only show two-letter country codes.

All in all, though, most of the emoji on this list continue to function across platforms.

How to Use the Unicode Characters

Unicode characters are universal encoding standards that assign a number to every character, emoji, or symbol.

To insert any of the emojis in HTML, for example, remove the U+ that all the characters start with, type in the hexadecimal character reference, add an ampersand (&), number sign (#) and x at the front, and a semi-colon at the end.

For example &#x1F923; would insert a "roll on the floor" emoji in HTML. I was able to escape the character from being displayed directly by wrapping backticks around it. Without wrapping backticks around the characters, I have this: 🤣.

For Unicode characters that have decimals, especially symbols, you can insert them in HTML without adding an x. For example &#8212; gives you an em dash (—).

For emojis with more than one unicode character, you have to include all the unicode characters and combine them with semi-colon(s) for it to work.

For instance, the US flag emoji has two unicode characters – U+1F1FA U+1F1F8. U+1F1FA alone won't work and U+1F1F8 alone won't work. But combining both will work – 🇺🇸. This is the code that made it work: &#x1F1FA;&#x1F1F8;

Same thing applies to the woman golfing emoji that has four total unicode characters – U+1F3CC, U+FE0F, U+200D, U+2640, and U+FE0F. None of them will work alone, but combinig all of them like this &#x1F3CC;&#xFE0F;&#x200D;&#x2640;&#xFE0F; leads to the emoji – 🏌️‍♀️
